MOHOBELO


Historical beginnings of mohobelo

The beginnings of mohobelo trace back to the period following the Lifaqane wars and the Great Trek.
In the late 1800s, despite the fact that much of the turmoil had calmed down, Basotho men continued
to protect outposts in the Maluti Mountains. They would engage in this for days continuously,
dedicating their time to singing, and over time, they started to include lively movements to complement
their voices. This occurred while attired in complete warrior gear – a blanket, shield, spear, or
knobkerrie (club) – and they started to utilize these weapons to highlight the power of their actions.


During this process, a fresh genre of dance music called mohobelo emerged within the Ha-Molapo community
in the northern district of Leribe. Vocally a blend of mokorotlo (war songs) and lelingoane or
mangae (initiation school chants), with significant praise-poetry elements, mohobelo is a dance of strides,
slides, and leaps solely executed by Basotho men. While it has territorial characteristics, as the dance styles
in the north vary from those in the south, it fundamentally serves as a demonstration of masculinity
with a naturally competitive element.


Village leaders quickly understood that the energy of mohobelo could be utilized effectively,
so community work teams (letsema) started incorporating the dance to invigorate
laborers during plowing and ho-pola (the process of harvesting wheat, millet, or corn). Historically,
women prepared beer and meals for the men laboring in the fields, incorporating ululations into the tunes and beats
of the mohobelo performers


When a man sought to move to a new village, it became a tradition for him to bring his koto (knob) to acquire land.
This would grant him the title of oa hoba, symbolizing his dedication to protect the village
during difficult times and to assist in cultivating the land and participating in the customs of mohobelo.
